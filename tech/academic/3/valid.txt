English[[28]edit]

Etymology[[29]edit]

   From [30]Middle French [31]valide (“healthy, sound, in good order”),
   from [32]Latin [33]validus, from [34]valeō (“I am strong, I am healthy,
   I am worth”) + [35]-idus, from [36]Proto-Indo-European [37]*wal (“be
   strong”).

Adjective[[38]edit]

   valid ([39]comparative [40]more valid, [41]superlative [42]most valid)
    1. Well [43]grounded or [44]justifiable, [45]pertinent.
          + 2012 March-April, [46]Jan Sapp, “Race Finished”, in
            [47]American Scientist^[48][1], volume 100, number 2, page
            164:

                      Few concepts are as emotionally charged as that of
                      race. The word conjures up a mixture of
                      associations—culture, ethnicity, genetics,
                      subjugation, exclusion and persecution. But is the
                      tragic history of efforts to define groups of people
                      by race really a matter of the misuse of science,
                      the abuse of a valid biological concept?

                I will believe him as soon as he offers a valid answer.

    2. [49]Acceptable, [50]proper or [51]correct.

                A valid format for the date is MM/DD/YY.
                Do not drive without a valid license.

    3. [52]Related to the current topic, or presented within context,
       [53]relevant.
    4. (logic) Of a [54]formula or [55]system: such that it evaluates to
       [56]true regardless of the input values.
    5. (logic) Of an [57]argument: whose [58]conclusion is always true
       whenever its [59]premises are true.

                An argument is valid if and only if the set consisting of
                both (1) all of its premises and (2) the contradictory of
                its conclusion is inconsistent.
