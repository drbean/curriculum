English[[20]edit]

Etymology[[21]edit]

   From [22]Middle English [23]proporcion, from [24]Old French
   [25]proportion, from [26]Latin [27]proportio (“comparative relation,
   proportion, symmetry, analogy”), from [28]pro (“for, before”) +
   [29]portio (“share, part”); see [30]portion.

Noun[[48]edit]

   proportion ([49]countable and [50]uncountable, plural [51]proportions)
    1. ([52]countable) A quantity of something that is part of the whole
       amount or number.
          + 1907, [53]Robert William Chambers, chapter [54]VI, in The
            Younger Set ([55]Project Gutenberg; EBook #14852), New York,
            N.Y.: [56]A. L. Burt Company, published 1 February 2005
            (Project Gutenberg version), [57]OCLC [58]4241346:

                      “I don't mean all of your friends—only a small
                      proportion—which, however, connects your circle with
                      that deadly, idle, brainless bunch—the insolent
                      chatterers at the opera, the gorged dowagers, the
                      worn-out, passionless men, the enervated matrons of
                      the summer capital, […]!”

    2. ([59]uncountable) Harmonious relation of parts to each other or to
       the whole.
    3. ([60]countable) Proper or equal share.
          + [61]Jeremy Taylor (1613–1677)

                      Let the women […] do the same things in their
                      proportions and capacities.

    4. The relation of one part to another or to the whole with respect to
       magnitude, quantity, or degree.

                the proportion of the parts of a building, or of the body

          + [62]Lancelot Ridley (ca.1500-1576)

                      The image of Christ, made after his own proportion.

          + Sir [63]Walter Scott (1771-1832)

                      Formed in the best proportions of her sex.

          + [64]Thomas Macaulay (1800-1859)

                      Documents are authentic and facts are true precisely
                      in proportion to the support which they afford to
                      his theory.

    5. ([65]mathematics, [66]countable) A [67]statement of [68]equality
       between two [69]ratios.
    6. ([70]countable, chiefly in the [71]plural) [72]Size.
          + 1898, [73]Winston Churchill, chapter 8, in [74]The Celebrity:

                      The humor of my proposition appealed more strongly
                      to Miss Trevor than I had looked for, and from that
                      time forward she became her old self again; […] .
                      Now she had come to look upon the matter in its true
                      proportions, and her anticipation of a possible
                      chance of teaching him a lesson was a pleasure to
                      behold.

          + 2012 May 20, Nathan Rabin, “[75]TV: Review: THE SIMPSONS
            (CLASSIC): “Marge Gets A Job” (season 4, episode 7; originally
            aired 11/05/1992)”, in The Onion AV Club:

                      What other television show would feature a
                      gorgeously designed sequence where a horrifically
                      mutated Pierre and Marie Curie, their bodies swollen
                      to Godzilla-like proportions from prolonged exposure
                      to the radiation that would eventually kill them,
                      destroy an Asian city with their bare hands like
                      vengeance-crazed monster-Gods?
