English[[23]edit]

Etymology[[24]edit]

   From [25]Middle English [26]remeven, [27]removen, from [28]Anglo-Norman
   [29]remover, [30]removeir, from [31]Old French [32]remouvoir, from
   [33]Latin [34]removēre, from [35]re- + [36]movēre (“to move”)

Pronunciation[[37]edit]

     * [38]IPA^([39]key): /ɹɪˈmuːv/
     *

       Audio (US)

       ([40]file)
     * Rhymes: [41]-uːv

Verb[[42]edit]

   remove (third-person singular simple present [43]removes, present
   participle [44]removing, simple past and past participle [45]removed)
    1. ([46]transitive) To [47]move something from one place to another,
       especially to [48]take away.

                He removed the marbles from the bag.

          + [49]Bible, [50]Deuteronomy xix.14:

                      Thou shalt not remove thy neighbour's landmark.

          + 1963, [51]Margery Allingham, chapter 2, in [52]The China
            Governess^[53][1]:

                      Now that she had rested and had fed from the
                      luncheon tray Mrs. Broome had just removed, she had
                      reverted to her normal gaiety.  She looked cool in a
                      grey tailored cotton dress with a terracotta scarf
                      and shoes and her hair a black silk helmet.

         1. ([54]obsolete, [55]formal) To replace a dish within a course.
               o 1959, [56]Georgette Heyer, chapter 1, in [57]The Unknown
                 Ajax:

                            But Richmond […] appeared to lose himself in
                            his own reflections. Some pickled crab, which
                            he had not touched, had been removed with a
                            damson pie; and his sister saw […] that he had
                            eaten no more than a spoonful of that either.

    2. ([58]transitive) To [59]murder.
    3. (cricket, [60]transitive) To [61]dismiss a batsman.
    4. ([62]transitive) To [63]discard, [64]set aside, especially
       something [65]abstract (a thought, feeling, etc.).
          + 1590, [66]Edmund Spenser, [67]The Faerie Queene, III.viii:

                      Die had she rather in tormenting griefe, / Then any
                      should of falsenesse her reproue, / Or loosenesse,
                      that she lightly did remoue.

          + 2013 June 21, [68]Karen McVeigh, “[69]US rules human genes
            can't be patented”, in [70]The Guardian Weekly, volume 189,
            number 2, page 10:

                      The US supreme court has ruled unanimously that
                      natural human genes cannot be patented, a decision
                      that scientists and civil rights campaigners said
                      removed a major barrier to patient care and medical
                      innovation.

    5. ([71]intransitive, now rare) To [72]depart, [73]leave.
          + 1485, Sir [74]Thomas Malory, [75]chapter vj, in [76]Le Morte
            Darthur, book V:

                      THenne the kynge dyd doo calle syre Gawayne / syre
                      Borce / syr Lyonel and syre Bedewere / and
                      commaunded them to goo strayte to syre Lucius / and
                      saye ye to hym that hastely he remeue oute of my
                      land / And yf he wil not / bydde hym make hym redy
                      to bataylle and not distresse the poure peple

    6. ([77]intransitive) To change one's [78]residence; to [79]move.
          + [80]William Shakespeare

                      Till Birnam wood remove to Dunsinane.

          + 1719, [81]Daniel Defoe, [82]Robinson Crusoe

                      Now my life began to be so easy that I began to say
                      to myself that could I but have been safe from more
                      savages, I cared not if I was never to remove from
                      the place where I lived.

          + 1834, [83]David Crockett, A Narrative of the Life of, Nebraska
            1987, p.20:

                      Shortly after this, my father removed, and settled
                      in the same county, about ten miles above
                      Greenville.

    7. To [84]dismiss or [85]discharge from [86]office.

                The President removed many postmasters.

Noun[[221]edit]

   remove (plural [222]removes)
    1. The act of [223]removing something.
          + (Can we [224]date this quote?) [225]Milton

                      This place should be at once both school and
                      university, not needing a remove to any other house
                      of scholarship.

          + (Can we [226]date this quote?) [227]Goldsmith

                      And drags at each remove a lengthening chain.

    2. (archaic) Removing a [228]dish at a [229]meal in order to replace
       it with the next [230]course, a dish thus replaced, or the
       replacement.
    3. ([231]Britain) (at some public schools) A [232]division of the
       [233]school, especially the [234]form prior to last
    4. A [235]step or [236]gradation (as in the phrase "at one remove")
          + (Can we [237]date this quote?) [238]Addison

                      A freeholder is but one remove from a legislator.

    5. [239]Distance in time or space; [240]interval.
          + 2007, James D. McCallister, King's Highway, [241]page 162:

                      In his unfortunate absence at this far remove of
                      2007, Zevon's musicianship and irascible wit are as
                      missed as ever.

    6. ([242]dated) The transfer of one's home or business to another
       place; a [243]move.
          + (Can we [244]date this quote?) [245]J. H. Newman

                      It is an English proverb that three removes are as
                      bad as a fire.

    7. The act of resetting a [246]horse's [247]shoe.

                (Can we [248]find and add a quotation of Jonathan Swift to
                this entry?)
